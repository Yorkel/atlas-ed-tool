Production System

Making AI Pipeline Choices Visible

Every AI pipeline makes choices that determine what it finds. These choices are invisible. AtlasED makes them visible and testable. This is a production NLP system analysing education policy documents across England—with every specification choice disclosed and every finding contestable.

Why This Matters This tool will be used to run workshops with policy stakeholders to discuss how AI outputs should be used in high-stakes decision-making. When a model tells you "SEND is the largest education issue," you need to know: is that real, or is that a choice I made when I set k=30?

Why NMF?

Model Choice Rationale
Transparency Over Performance

I chose Non-negative Matrix Factorization (NMF) deliberately—not because it's the most sophisticated model, but because this project is about specification transparency, and NMF makes every modeling choice explicit and auditable.

The number of topics (k), the vocabulary preprocessing, the document weighting—these aren't hidden inside a neural network. They're parameters you set, and you can see what happens when you change them.

This approach scales. Today it's NMF on education documents. Tomorrow it could be transformer embeddings, RAG pipelines, or multimodal models—the principle stays the same: disclose the specification choices, show what changes when you perturb them, and let the user decide.

The Core Finding No specification choice preserves more than 18% of topic names. Standard diagnostics cannot detect this. The only way to know is to perturb and compare. That's what the next pages show.

Future Vision

From Demonstration to Methodology

This dashboard demonstrates specification sensitivity in one pipeline. The goal is to extend this approach into a formal tool that can be used in every pipeline, every domain, every high-stakes decision system.

The Vision: SpecCheck as a Standard

From AtlasED to Universal Practice

SpecCheck becomes an open-source library with three standardized metrics, applicable to any analytical pipeline, running as a pre-deployment check, a CI job, or a runtime logging layer.

For Engineers: "Run SpecCheck before deployment" becomes as routine as "run tests." The library outputs a specification sensitivity report—a PDF or dashboard showing how findings vary across perturbations.

For Institutions: Every deployed model includes a SpecCheck disclosure—a public statement of the specification choices made and the sensitivity of findings to those choices.

For Affected Users: The basis to ask: "Would the decision have been different under a different specification?" This is contestability at the infrastructure level.

Why This Benefits Everyone

For Researchers Specification sensitivity is not only a quality check. It is a discovery method. Every perturbation shown in this dashboard produced findings about the education system that were inaccessible from any single model run.
For Institutions Auditable evidence that specification choices were tested. When a decision is contested, the institution can show: "We tested this under five different specifications. Here's what changed and what stayed robust."
For Those Affected by Decisions The basis to ask whether the decision would have been different under a different specification. This is the foundation of meaningful AI contestability—not just "explain the output," but "show me what would change if the choices were different."

Next Steps

From Demonstration to Deployment

Immediate: Use this dashboard in stakeholder workshops to demonstrate the principle. Gather feedback from policymakers, engineers, and affected communities.

Short-term: Develop the SpecCheck library with three core metrics: specification reproducibility, finding stability, and disclosure completeness.

Long-term: Establish SpecCheck as a standard practice in high-stakes AI deployment— alongside fairness testing, adversarial robustness, and interpretability.
